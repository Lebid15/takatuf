I– Read the following text then do the tasks below:
   Culture Shock: Diana's Diary

   Diana, a student from Aleppo, Syria, is studying in Montreal, Canada. The following passages are taken from her diary.

   Moving to a country with a very different climate could be a challenge! In Syria we have a Mediterranean weather, with mild, rainy winters. Here in Canada, winter is very cold and snowy. The temperature average is between single digits and -20°C. I was very worried about the cold. But it wasn't a problem because most buildings here are well heated. Actually this city knows how to stay warm. When the weather gets too chilly, I head to the Underground City — a series of interconnected tunnels beneath Montreal which run for over 32 km. The tunnels connect shopping malls, universities, banks and seven metro stations.

   I miss my mom's cooking a lot. The food here is delicious but it's not really the same. Foods typically considered national dishes of Canada include poutine, Montreal bagels and butter tarts. My most amazing experience was Sugar Time, usually at the end of March. It is the season organised in 'la cabane à sucre', a place where maple sap is boiled to produce maple syrup. It is heated above 100 degrees, and the drops are placed on the snow, becoming like a candy on a stick!

   My overall experience during these six months taught me to avoid quick judgements and try to understand the point of view of people in another culture. I'm staying with a Quebecois family who are teaching me a lot about Montreal. They told me not to make quick decisions, and not to leave right away even if I wanted to. They were right! People don't take the time to adapt, so they miss out on wonderful experiences.

   Culture shock is the feeling of confusion, discomfort and disorientation that people may experience when they move to a new country or cultural environment. It is a natural response to the unfamiliar customs, values, language, and behaviours of a different society. Almost everyone who travels or moves abroad experiences some degree of culture shock.

   Culture shock typically progresses through four stages. The first is the 'honeymoon stage', during which everything seems exciting and new. The second is the 'frustration stage', when the differences between the new culture and one's own become difficult to deal with. The third is the 'adjustment stage', when the person begins to understand and adapt to the new culture. The fourth and final stage is the 'acceptance stage', when the person feels comfortable and at home in the new environment.

   There are several strategies that can help people overcome culture shock. Learning the local language, even at a basic level, can significantly reduce the feeling of isolation. Building relationships with local people helps one to understand the culture from the inside. Maintaining contact with family and friends back home also provides emotional support.

   It is important to remember that culture shock is a temporary experience. With patience, an open mind, and a willingness to learn, most people successfully adapt to their new environment and even come to appreciate the richness of a different culture.

X– Composition: (50 marks)
Q40. Write a composition of no less than 80 words on the following topic:

"Write about how people can adapt to living in a new country or culture and what benefits they can gain from this experience."
Moving to a new country can be one of the most exciting — and challenging — experiences in a person's life. Culture shock is a natural part of this journey, but with the right attitude, anyone can adapt successfully and benefit greatly from the experience.

The first step is to be open-minded and patient. It takes time to understand a new culture, and making quick judgements can lead to misunderstandings. Learning the local language, even at a basic level, is also very helpful as it allows better communication and helps build friendships with local people. Exploring local traditions, trying new foods, and participating in community activities are wonderful ways to understand and appreciate the new culture.

The benefits of living abroad are enormous. People develop greater independence, self-confidence, and problem-solving skills. They also gain a broader perspective on the world and become more tolerant and understanding of people who are different from themselves. In conclusion, while adapting to a new culture requires effort and patience, the personal growth and enrichment that result from the experience are truly invaluable.
