I– Read the following text then do the tasks below:
   UNICEF

   The United Nations Children's Fund (UNICEF), originally known as the United Nations International Children's Emergency Fund, is responsible for providing humanitarian and developmental aid to children worldwide. It has won the Nobel Prize, the first to be awarded to an organisation. Despite being highly active in fundraising, awareness, relief work and research, very few people know the origin of the organisation.

   UNICEF is supported entirely by the voluntary contributions of governments, non-governmental organisations, foundations, corporations and private individuals, and receives no funding from the assessed dues of the United Nations. Most of the fundraising is done by UNICEF's 36 national committees and the voluntary support of millions of people around the world.

   UNICEF was created in 1946 and began with a definite mission of providing emergency food and healthcare to children in the countries that had been destroyed by World War II. In 1953, its original name was changed to the United Nations Children's Fund to reflect its broader mission, but it retained the original acronym after being officially adopted as a permanent branch of the UN in 1954.

   The agency operates in over 190 countries, focusing on the welfare of children in at-risk areas. Since 2006, the organisation has concentrated on key issues including child survival and development, basic education and gender equality, child protection and policy advocacy, and partnerships.

   At last, UNICEF is working to create a world in which no child is ever bought or sold. UNICEF believes that every child deserves to grow up in a loving family and supports inter-country adoption when conducted ethically. At the same time, UNICEF works to support families in need so that no one is ever forced by poverty to give up a child.

   The United Nations was established by the Charter of the United Nations, which was signed on 26 June 1945 by the representatives of 50 countries including Syria. There were 51 founding members in 1945. The United Nations officially came into existence on 24 October 1945 after ratification of the Charter. This day is now celebrated each year around the world as United Nations Day.

   The Security Council contains five permanent members: China, France, the Russian Federation, the United Kingdom, and the United States, and ten non-permanent members elected for two-year terms by the General Assembly. The UN also has regional offices in Geneva, Vienna, and Nairobi, and its official languages are Arabic, Chinese, English, French, Russian, and Spanish.

   The United Nations was the second multipurpose international organisation established in the 20th century. Its predecessor, the League of Nations, was created by the Treaty of Versailles in 1919 and disbanded in 1946.

   At the beginning of the 21st century, the UN has struggled to address humanitarian crises and civil wars, unprecedented refugee flows, and global health emergencies. Despite these challenges, the UN remains the world's most important international organisation, dedicated to maintaining peace and promoting human rights worldwide.

X– Composition: (50 marks)
Q40. Write a composition of no less than 80 words on the following topic:

"Write about the importance of international organisations such as the United Nations in maintaining world peace and protecting human rights."
International organisations such as the United Nations play a crucial role in today's world. They bring nations together to work towards common goals, including peace, security, and the protection of human rights. Without such organisations, the world would be a far more dangerous and divided place.

The United Nations has achieved many important goals since its establishment in 1945. Its agencies, such as UNICEF, UNHCR, and WHO, provide humanitarian aid to millions of people in need. The Security Council works to resolve conflicts and prevent wars, while the General Assembly serves as a forum for dialogue and cooperation among nations.

In conclusion, despite its limitations, the United Nations remains an essential pillar of the international order. It represents humanity's best effort to build a world based on dialogue, justice, and mutual respect. Every nation has a responsibility to support and strengthen this vital institution.
