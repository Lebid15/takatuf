I– Read the following text then do the tasks below:
   Famous Failures Who Refused to Give Up

   One of the most destructive moments in our lives is failure. When we work hard and keep faith with clear heart and soul to accomplish something and fail, it will surely have negative consequences for our lives. When we fail, life turns upside down. Everything seems to disappear into thin air. However, most people don't realise that failure acts as a stepping-stone towards success. They don't know that we can learn more about our lives when we fail. Throughout history, there have been many famous people who had once the greatest failures in their lives, but later they could learn from their experience and were able to reach the top.

   Thomas Edison is by far one of the most famous inventors in history. He holds 1,093 patents to his name. However, when attempting to invent a commercially-viable electric lightbulb, he failed over 10,000 times. When asked by a reporter how it felt to fail so many times, he merely stated, "I have not failed 10,000 times. I have not failed once. I have succeeded in proving that those 10,000 ways will not work. When I have eliminated the ways that will not work, I will find the way that will work."

   Albert Einstein, one of the greatest thinkers of our time, didn't speak until he was four years old. He also failed his entrance exam to the Swiss Federal Polytechnic school in Zurich at sixteen. Even his father, up until the time of his death, considered his son to be a major failure. After eventually graduating from college, Einstein worked as an insurance salesman, but quit after some time because he failed at that as well.

   Walt Disney is the creative genius who brought us Mickey Mouse, Donald Duck and Snow White. Yet he faced many failures. His first company, Laugh-O-Gram, went bankrupt. It wasn't until 5 years later — after he created Mickey Mouse — that he began to experience success and fame.

II– Read the following text then do the tasks below:
   Many people believe that success depends mainly on talent or intelligence. However, research suggests otherwise. Psychologist Angela Duckworth introduced the concept of "grit" — a combination of passion and perseverance for long-term goals. Her studies showed that in many fields, grit was a stronger predictor of success than intelligence or talent alone.

   People with grit do not simply rely on their natural abilities; they work consistently and keep going even when faced with difficulties. Duckworth found that graduates who scored high on grit measures were more likely to complete challenging programmes than those who had higher academic scores but less determination.

   Another key concept is the "growth mindset", a term coined by psychologist Carol Dweck. People with a growth mindset believe that their abilities can be developed through effort and learning. They see challenges as opportunities to improve, and they treat mistakes as valuable lessons rather than signs of failure.

   In conclusion, both grit and a growth mindset are more important than natural talent in determining long-term success. Anyone who is willing to work hard, stay focused, and learn from mistakes has the potential to succeed.
